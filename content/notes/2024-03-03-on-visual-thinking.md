---
title: "On Visual Thinking"
date: 2024-03-03
topics: [design]
summary: "Images don't decorate writing. They carry arguments that sentences handle badly: spatial relationships, scale, texture, the thing itself."
---

There is a class of argument that prose cannot make efficiently. Describing the layout of a grid system in words is possible but tedious — a diagram communicates it in a glance. Explaining what a particular typeface looks like at display size requires showing it. Arguing that two colors clash is harder than putting them next to each other.

Writers trained primarily in text tend to reach for images too late, as illustration of something already described. The better use is as primary evidence — putting the image first and letting the prose draw conclusions from what the reader can already see.

The caption is underused as a form. A good caption doesn't repeat what's visible in the image; it points to something the reader might not have noticed, or names what the image is evidence of. The image shows; the caption argues.
