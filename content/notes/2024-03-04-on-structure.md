summary: "A heading is a contract with the reader. It promises that everything below it belongs together and leads somewhere."
---

The test for a heading is whether you could reconstruct the argument from headings alone. If your headings are "Introduction," "Methods," "Discussion" — the structure carries no information. If they are specific enough to be claims, the skeleton of the piece is visible before the reader has read a word.

This matters most when readers are scanning, which is nearly always. Web readers especially move through text non-linearly, jumping to the section that addresses their question. A heading that names a section precisely allows this navigation; a vague one forces full reading to locate the relevant part.

The deeper discipline is getting the sections right in the first place. Headings that feel awkward to name are usually symptoms of a section that hasn't earned its existence — content that belongs to an adjacent section or hasn't been thought through. The resistance you feel when naming a section is diagnostic.
