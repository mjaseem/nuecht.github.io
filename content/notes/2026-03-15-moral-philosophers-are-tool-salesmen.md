This is an attempt to pen down my positions on morality.

## The moral question

I have some instinct demanding all my actions be rated on a scale of good and bad. I often have answers to it. Sometimes I don't and it can keep me up at night. Most everyone seems to have this to some degree. Noted exceptions are psychopaths. So we can consider the moral question an anthropological reality. The answers? Maybe not so much.

The rest of this article might seem a fool's errand to those not affected by moral questions. I can relate to this. There are a thousand books<sup>[citation needed]</sup> written about meaning of life. They try to address the monumental challenge of addressing your mind's need for meaning while not being able to rationally provide one. I don't feel the need for meaning and this all seems quite exhausting and unnecessary. That's not the case with morality though. I am willing to spend some money and time on it. If you've got some cool ideas, I might even subscribe to your substack.

## Where does it come from and does it matter?

We likely evolved moral instincts as a cooperation mechanism. The tribes who didn't find betraying their kin abhorrent died out and those who did are your ancestors.

Do they matter still? As a cooperation mechanism they still have a lot of value. But you could deduce the useful bits rationally and just go with that instead of deferring to morality. Even then it would be more effective in supporting the reasoning. It is a difficult ask for reason alone to counter all our instincts for self interest.


## Answers

I don't believe in absolute or transcendental morality. Their existence is a factual question about nature. I have looked for it in nature and I am not finding it. If I could find some absolutes in my mind, I could have taken that as some evidence, even if a weak one, because I am very much part of nature, but it's not there.

While not absolute and definitely not completely satisfying, the fundamental genetically inherited instincts have answers to some basic moral questions. Killing an innocent person seems pretty terrible and I don't need to read any bible for that. But is it bad to build software infrastructure for a company who sells it to another company whose CEO was pictured playing golf with dictators? The brain cells that flinched at murder didn't parse past "software infrastructure".

Culture has some answers too. Much of my sense of right and wrong I inherited from my mom. Some I got from my friends and some I absorbed from the books I read as a kid. Culture gets me a bit further than purely genetically inherited instincts.

This still doesn't give you a complete system. It's as if evolution gave you a need, some basic building blocks and asked you to fill up the rest using cultural and mental faculties. 

Given the convoluted set of requirements posed by all your cultural, genetic cravings, deciding what is right or wrong becomes a hard problem. You want to satisfy those requirements, but you don't want to freeze every time there's a new situation your moral framework failed to address.

## Moral philosophers are tool-salesmen

This is an engineering problem. You have a set of constraints and you want a framework that fits it well enough.

This is where religious founders and moral philosophers come in. They sell you a framework, you can see if it's a fit. If not, there's a hundred more out there. You don't have to do the work of building the framework up from scratch. As a bonus, you don't have to convince yourself to take the framework seriously. There's a bearded man doing miracles telling you this, so he must be right. Or a man in a suit who knows a lot of words and may have a PhD if that's more to your taste.


Acknowledging the meta question of what is the moral compass and finding "it's some evolutionary instinct" as an answer does make finding the answer to the more basic question of "is this action good or bad" difficult. You have to admit the answer is going to be made up yet you can't not answer it. 

This is where I find myself. I want to take the time to shop for philosophers who see through the conflict, worked out its consequences and came to robust conclusions. 


As an aside: I am absolutely annoyed that many philosophers I have had the chance to read can't seem to address these fundamental questions without first making up a bunch of words as abstractions and then talking only in terms of the abstract. They seem to get lazy while connecting the abstract back to the reality. You as a reader have to do that work yourself, while you are still not sure if you actually understood the abstractions the author setup. You forgot what 'Moral logisitivism' means? Time to flip back a hundred pages to the chapter that you skimmed through because you couldn't think how four pages of jargon could be worth reading.
