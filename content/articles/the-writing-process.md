Writing happens in three stages that resist being collapsed into one. The first is generative: getting ideas out without evaluating them. The second is structural: finding the argument buried in the draft and excavating it. The third is editorial: cutting everything that doesn't serve the argument.{{< sidenote ref="2024-03-04-on-structure" >}}

Most writers spend too long in the first stage and too little time in the second. The structural pass — where you ask *what is this actually about?* and *what order does it need to be in?* — is where writing becomes good. It often requires cutting the first third of a draft, which is usually warm-up.

## The Draft

Write the first draft at speed, without editing. Stopping to fix a sentence breaks the forward momentum that lets the larger structure emerge. The quality of individual sentences doesn't matter yet; what matters is getting all the pieces on the table.

A useful constraint: set a time limit for the first draft, not a word count. Word counts invite padding; time limits encourage speed.

## The Revision

Revision is not proofreading. It is structural surgery. Read the draft and ask:

1. What is the single thing this piece argues?
2. Does every section advance that argument?
3. Are sections in the order that the reader needs, or the order the writer discovered them?

The answer to the third question is almost always: the order of discovery. Reverse it.

## On Cutting

> Kill your darlings.
>
> — William Faulkner (attr.)

The sentence you're most attached to is usually the one that most needs to go. Attachment signals that the sentence is doing work for you — justifying the research, demonstrating cleverness — rather than work for the reader.

A practical test: remove the sentence and read the paragraph. If nothing is lost, the sentence was lost already.
